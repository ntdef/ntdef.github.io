Frogs and Flies Pt.2: Markov Chains for Fun and for Profit!

Date Tue 17 March 2015 Tags puzzles / graph theory / numpy / markov chains / probability

In my last post, I talked about using graphs and simulations to solve a problem of a frog and a fly traversing a grid until they collide. Both the agents could only walk on a restricted path, which we modeled using a graph that connected valid moves. The simulation was great and all, but how do we prove that our results are correct?

We use Markov Chains of course!

Markov Chains, but in a way that won't make you cry buckets of tears.

Now I realize that the mere mention of Markov Chains makes some people shudder. To others, it just sounds like the name of a cheap vodka. But really Markov Chains are not all that complicated: basically a Markov Chain describes how a system might evolve over time when we know the probabilities of how the system might change given that it was in a certain state before.

It's best explained with an example.

Suppose you wanted to know how my Netflix viewing habits evolve over time. After I'm done watching a show, what am I likely to watch the next time I'm on Netflix? And the next time? And the time after that? And the time after that? And the time after that? ad nauseum

Markov Chains, House of Cards and You

Say that I'm watching House of Cards and once the episode is over, there's a 60% chance that I'll watch the next episode. You leave and say you're going check in on me tomorrow around the same time, which leaves me time to watch, say, 8 episodes of any show. What's the probability that I'll still be watching House of Cards when you get back, given that I'll watch another epsiode of the same series 60% of the time?

Well, we could give a function \(p(t) = p_0^t\) where \(p_0\) is the probability of watching the same show after I finish this one, given that I started watching House of Cards. This is essentially what a Markov Chain is: a series of states with a certain proability of transition between them. The idea is to start at time 0 and see how a state evolves over time. In fact, Netflix does this kind of analysis all the time, and they, quite famously, found that people generally tend to binge watch shows on Netflix.

Usually Markov chains are given in a matrix form:

$$ \begin{bmatrix} .60 & .20 \\ .40 & .80 \end{bmatrix} $$

The entries are the probabilties of going from the row state to the column state. To contimue the example above, row 1, column 1, is the probability of watching HoC again. and row 2, column 1 is the proability of watching something else, given that I'm watching HoC. On the other hand row 2, column 1 gives the proability that I'll be watching House of Cards next time, given that I was watching somethin else, and likewise for row 2, column 2.

In the case of the frog and fly problem, we can give similar conditional proabilities for going from one cell to another.

First, lets convert our grap to a stochastic matrix. A stochastic matrix is a matrix represenation of a markov chain where the entries represent the probability of going from state i to state j. In our case, the stochasic matrix represents the probability of our critter moving from a cell to any other cell.

We'll write a function that will give equal weights to moving over each edge of our graph and return the desired matrix.

def convert_to_stochastic(graph):
    new_graph = nx.DiGraph()
    new_graph.add_nodes_from(graph)
    for node in graph.nodes():
        n = graph.degree(node)
        for nbr in graph.neighbors(node):
            new_graph.add_edge(node, nbr, weight=1.0/n)
    return nx.to_numpy_matrix(new_graph, sorted(new_graph.nodes())).T

# SO note that for this to make sense, you need to make it geometric. 
# The intersection at time t means time at first intersection, which means you have to 
# compute the probability of not intersecting until time t, which is a geometric random variable.

Let $Y_{ij}(t)$ be the probability of the fly being at the $ij$-th cell at time t, and let $X_{ij}(t)$ be likewise for the frog for $i$ in $\{A,B,C\}$ and $j$ in $\{1,2,3\}$. Let $p$ be a vector where the $i$th entry is 1 for the starting cell of the critter and 0 otherwise, and let $M$ be the stochastic transition matrix. We can compute the probability of being in the $ij$th cell at time $t$ is given by the $ij$the entry of the vector $q(t)$, where $$q(t) = M^{t}p$$.

Since $X$ and $Y$ are independent, the probability of both being at the $ij$ cell at time $t$ is $Z_{ij}(t) = X_{ij}(t)Y_{ij}(t)$ and we have $$ Z_{ij}(t) = q_X(t)q_Y(t)$$.

def dont_collide_at(t):
    if t <> 0:
        return np.multiply(1 - collide_at(t), dont_collide_at(t - 1))
    else: 
        return 1 - collide_at(1)
